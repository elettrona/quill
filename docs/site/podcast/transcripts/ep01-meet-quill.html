Liam: Welcome to The QUILL Cast, the show where we explore QUILL, the screen-reader-first writing studio built by the people who actually depend on it. I'm Liam.

Jessica: And I'm Jessica. Over this series we're going to walk through everything QUILL can do, from your very first keystroke to building audiobooks, linked note vaults, and rescuing scanned documents nobody else can read.

Liam: Let's start with the big question. Jessica, what is QUILL?

Jessica: QUILL is a writing environment for Windows and Mac that was designed from the first line of code for people who work by ear and by keyboard. Most editors bolt accessibility on at the end. QUILL flips that. The screen reader experience is the primary experience, and everything else is built around it.

Liam: And that shows up in some very concrete design promises. The first one is that QUILL owns the essentials. The editor, focus, undo, and announcements always behave predictably. No mystery focus jumps, no dialogs you can't escape from, no actions you can't undo.

Jessica: The second promise is that everything beyond the core is optional and off by default. Artificial intelligence, cloud services, folder watching, extensions. QUILL is a fast, quiet text editor until you decide you want more.

Liam: And the third promise, which I honestly wish more software made: nothing leaves your computer without asking you. Every single network call in QUILL is tracked in an internal audit, and anything that would upload your document tells you first, in plain language, and waits for your answer.

Jessica: So who is QUILL for? Obviously blind and low-vision writers are the core audience. But it has grown into a genuinely great editor for anyone who prefers the keyboard: authors organizing novels, students converting course readings, podcasters producing audio, people taking meeting minutes from recordings.

Liam: And it's free. Completely free. There's an optional donations page, but every feature is available to everyone, forever. That matters, because so much assistive technology historically came with a four-figure price tag.

Jessica: Let's talk about the first five minutes. You install QUILL from a normal Windows installer, you launch it, and a short setup wizard asks a couple of questions about how you like to work. That picks a feature profile for you.

Liam: Profiles are worth explaining because they're one of QUILL's best ideas. A profile is a named set of feature switches. If you pick a simple writing profile, the menus stay small and focused. If you pick the full experience, you get everything. And you can change your mind any time in Preferences under Profiles and Features.

Jessica: The philosophy is that no one should have to navigate menus full of features they'll never use. A screen reader reads menus top to bottom. Every item you don't need is a second of your life, every time.

Liam: Once you're in, you're in a tabbed editor. Each document is a tab, control tab switches between them, and the status bar announces what matters, when it matters, at a verbosity you control. Type, save with control S, done. It is, deliberately, a completely ordinary editor at its heart.

Jessica: Let's give people a taste of what's ahead in this series. Liam, rapid fire. What are your three favorite QUILL features?

Liam: Only three? Okay. One, the command palette. Control shift P, type a few letters of what you want, press enter. Every command in the whole application, searchable. You never have to memorize a menu path again.

Jessica: That's episode two territory.

Liam: Two, the speech suite. QUILL can read your document aloud in everything from classic DECtalk to modern neural voices, it can take dictation completely offline, and it can transcribe a two-hour recording into a document, privately, on your own machine.

Jessica: Episode five. And number three?

Liam: The Accessible Vault. Linked notes with backlinks, like the fancy sighted-people note apps, except designed to be heard instead of seen. What links here, spoken as a list. It genuinely changed how I organize research. What about you?

Jessica: I'll cheat and name new arrivals. This release, QUILL added a document rescue tool that takes a scanned PDF, the kind that's just photographs of pages, and turns it into real text using free, on-device recognition. Nothing gets uploaded, nothing costs money, and QUILL asks before every step. And GLOW, the accessibility review system, is now fully unlocked. It audits your documents for accessibility problems and fixes them with your approval.

Liam: Those are episodes four and nine. We've got a whole season here.

Jessica: Before we wrap, let's set expectations for how to follow along. Every episode is hands-on. Have QUILL open while you listen. When we say a keystroke, try it. When we mention a menu, walk to it. The transcripts for every episode are published alongside the audio, so you can review anything in braille or with your screen reader at your own pace.

Liam: And one habit to build from day one: press F1. Context-sensitive help works almost everywhere in QUILL. Whatever dialog or control you're sitting in, F1 tells you what it is and how to use it.

Jessica: Next time, we go deep on getting around: the command palette, the QUILL key, quick navigation, and how to rebind every keystroke in the app to fit your hands.

Liam: Until then, I'm Liam.

11. Trademark and domain clearance — the gate (DO THIS FIRST)

Do we even need this for an open-source project? (Yes — but cheaply.)

A free/MIT project does not get a pass on trademark. Why:

  • Trademark turns on "use in commerce" (broadly read — distributing free software with a website, downloads, and app-store listings counts) and likelihood of confusion. You do not have to charge money to be using a name in a way trademark law cares about.
  • The risk for a free project is not owing damages — it is being forced to rename by a prior holder's cease-and-desist. An injunction (a "stop using it" order) does not require that you profited.
  • Open-source licenses cover copyright, not the name. MIT/GPL explicitly tend to exclude trademarks. (Debian shipped Firefox as "Iceweasel" for years purely over Mozilla's trademark, even though the code was free.)
  • App stores and domain registrars have their own takedown/dispute processes that do not care whether you profit.

What being free / open-source genuinely changes: you can skip registering your own mark (registration is optional), and you can clear the name for $0 with a DIY knockout. What it does not change: you should still run that free knockout before building brand and community — which is exactly what this project is doing (app stores, a website, marketing to the blind community, consolidating other products under the name). The expensive part is optional; the cheap check is not.

(This is general principle, not legal advice.)

Web research narrows the field; it does not clear a name. Before any public use of TINDRA:

  1. USPTO Class 9 search (computer software) + the specific goods (word-processing / accessibility software), ideally via a trademark attorney — plus international classes if you sell outside the US.
  2. Domain — the exact-match names are already taken. Verified via WHOIS: tindra.com is registered (since 2006, IONOS, transfer-locked) and tindra.org is registered (since 2015, Key-Systems, owner redacted); tindra.store is the furniture brand and the known "Tindra" brands also hold tindra-design.com and tindrah.com. "Tindra" is also a common Swedish given name, so exact-match domains across TLDs are largely claimed.
    • tindra.app is now taken by a live commercial software product (see the June 2026 update at the top of this section), so it is no longer an option despite being the earlier recommendation.
    • Options: (a) pursue a broker purchase of tindra.com/.org if the budget and a willing seller exist (premium, uncertain) — confirm with an authoritative registrar WHOIS first; or (b) go prefixed/alternate and own it cleanly: usetindra.com, gettindra.com / .org, tindra.io, or tindra.studio. For a community/nonprofit publisher (Community Access), a prefixed .org (e.g., gettindra.org) reads well and is far cheaper than chasing the 2006-era .com — but a prefixed domain does not cure the same-class software collision that the tindra.app product creates; resolve the trademark question (or pivot) first.
    • A free domain is not the same as a clear trademark — keep step 1 (Class 9 search) as the real gate; lock matching socials and the GitHub org alongside.
  3. App stores / package registries: check the Microsoft Store, Mac App Store, npm/PyPI for tindra collisions.
  4. Defensive filing: once cleared, file the mark in the relevant classes before launch; secure Sparks (or the chosen extension name) too if distinctive.

Suggested vendors (starting points, not an endorsement)

Verify current fees, reviews, and that you are working with a licensed U.S. trademark attorney before engaging. Match the vendor to the goal: for a real go/no-go and a defensible launch you want a comprehensive search plus an attorney opinion, not just a filing service.

1. Do this yourself first (free preliminary "knockout"):

  • USPTO trademark search — search.uspto.gov (the official tool that replaced TESS). Search the exact mark and close variants in Class 9 / 42.
  • Trademarkia — free public search for a quick read of existing marks.
  • Domains / handles — a registrar (Namecheap, Cloudflare, GoDaddy) for an available alternate (usetindra.com, gettindra.org, etc. — tindra.app is taken); Namechk or similar for social handles.

2. Flat-fee trademark attorneys (the recommended path — search + opinion + file):

  • Gerben IP (gerbenlaw.com) — well-established; flat ~$3,000 covers a comprehensive federal/state/common-law search, an attorney opinion letter, and filing in up to two classes (plus USPTO fees). Good all-in option.
  • Dawsey IP — ~$850 flat for a comprehensive U.S. search; single-class filing free of attorney fee (you still pay the ~$350/class USPTO fee). Strong value for the search step.
  • Flat Fee Trademark (flatfeetrademark.com), Knowmad Law, The Trademark Firm, Counsel for Creators, Cognition IP (startup-focused) — other reputable flat-fee attorney options worth quoting.

3. Budget DIY-with-filing (cheapest, but shallowest — use with caution):

  • Trademark Engine, LegalZoom, Trademarkia (filing service), Bizee, Rocket Lawyer — these file inexpensively, but their "search" is typically shallow and they do not give a real clearance opinion. Fine for a low-stakes mark; not a substitute for an attorney clearance before a rebrand investment.

4. Comprehensive search-report firms (usually accessed through your attorney):

  • Corsearch and CompuMark (Clarivate) — the professional full-search reports an attorney orders and interprets. You rarely buy these directly.

5. Finding / vetting an attorney:

  • INTA (International Trademark Association) member directory; your state bar referral service; reviews on Avvo / Martindale.

Recommended sequence: start with a ~$300–$850 attorney knockout or comprehensive search (e.g., Dawsey's ~$850 comprehensive, or a knockout from a flat-fee firm) → if it comes back clean, proceed to the opinion + filing (Gerben's bundled ~$3,000, or search + per-class filing elsewhere) → register alternate domains in parallel. Spend nothing on rebrand or premium domains until the knockout passes.

If clearance fails, the runner-up process is the same: pick the next distinctive coinage and re-run steps 1–4. Do not skip this for speed.

15. Extensions naming: TINDRA Sparks

"Quillins" should probably retire with QUILL rather than being awkwardly reshaped into "Tindrins." The best replacement is TINDRA Sparks.

An individual plugin is a Spark. Collectively, they are TINDRA Sparks.

It fits the TINDRA identity (twinkle / shine) and gives an entire vocabulary:

  • Extensions — the plain-language technical term
  • TINDRA Sparks — the friendly branded name
  • Spark Gallery — where users discover extensions
  • Spark Manager — where users install, update, enable, and remove them
  • Spark SDK — developer tooling
  • Spark Pack — a curated bundle
  • Create a Spark — developer documentation
  • Featured Sparks — recommended extensions

The interface can preserve clarity by combining both terms:

Extensions: TINDRA Sparks Add new abilities to TINDRA with extensions called Sparks.

That dual framing matters for accessibility and localization. A new user immediately understands what the feature does, while the brand still feels magical.

Naming conventions to adopt

  • Repos / packages: lowercase, hyphenated, type-prefixed — tindra-spark-*, tindra-companion-*, tindra-theme-*, tindra-language-pack-<code>, tindra-spark-pack-*.
  • Python imports: lowercase, underscored namespace packages — tindra_sparks.*, tindra_companions.*.
  • Manifest: id is publisher.short-name (e.g. community-access.markdown-tools); type is the machine category; optional displayType is the human label shown in the Gallery; tindraVersion is a semver range gating compatibility.
  • User-facing names: never embed "Spark" in the extension's own name; the Gallery composes "Spark by " so names don't stutter.

Sequencing and back-compat

  1. Phase 1 (surface): display strings only — title bar, About, menus, wizard, Gallery copy show TINDRA and Sparks. No identifier changes; fully reversible.
  2. Phase 2 (Sparks ecosystem): rename quillins_bundled → sparks_bundled, add the new type values and namespaces, ship the manifest migrator, keep the quillin alias and legacy-manifest loader. Old extensions still load.
  3. Phase 3 (deep identifiers, major version): quill → tindra package, AppId, data-dir migration, quill.exe → tindra.exe, credential labels, @quill/api → @tindra/api — all guided, announced, and reversible.

Definition of done / CI gates to update

  • GATE-VC (version consistency): teach it the new product_name and any renamed installer fields; it must still agree across build/version.toml, __init__.py, the iss, and CHANGELOG.
  • iss generator + sync test: regenerate tindra.iss from build_inno_setup_script; the committed-iss-in-sync test must pass for the new identity.
  • Spark lint / manifest schema tests: accept both quillin and spark manifests until the alias window closes, then drop quillin.
  • Module/import-boundary checks: update any quill-prefixed path assertions to tindra; keep a temporary quill re-export shim covered by a deprecation test.
  • Docs parity: README, user guide, PRD, release notes, and the Spark SDK docs all use TINDRA / Spark vocabulary; old doc URLs 301 to the new ones.

Reminder: build/version.toml is the single human-edited source of truth for release identity, and it is local-only (gitignored). Drive every generated name (installer, build-info module, display strings) from it — do not hard-code the product or channel name in committed code.

17. Alternate names (fallback if TINDRA is blocked)

Because the tindra.app same-class collision puts a TINDRA launch on uphill footing (section 11), keep a vetted shortlist ready. These are acronym/ backronym candidates whose letters genuinely map to the product, chosen to be pronounceable and easy to spell with a screen reader, and leaning on the same light/clarity heritage. None are cleared — each still needs the section 11 USPTO Class 9/42 + domain check before use. Collision flags below are preliminary, not a clearance.

Top tier

Name Backronym Theme / why Collision risk
AVRA Accessible · Voice · Reading · Authoring Coined, short, distinctive, trivially spellable (AV-rah) Low-ish — other uses are a Greek given name / restaurant (unrelated class); verify software
VERBA Voice · Editing · Reading · Braille · Access Latin for "words" — literally on-point for a writing studio (VER-bah) Low-medium; verify
LUMA Local · Universal · Multimodal · Access Maps to three brand pillars; luminance/"shine" carries the TINDRA light motif (LOO-mah) Higher — Luma AI (video), luma.com (events); verify hard

Runners-up

Name Backronym Theme / why Collision risk
SONARA Speech · Offline · Narration · Authoring · Reading · Access Sound/listening theme; all six letters true Medium — a "Sonara AI" may exist; check
VELA Voice · Editing · Local · Access Constellation ("the sails") — light/navigation Medium — pronunciation ambiguity (VEE-la/VAY-la); some game/brand uses
CLARA Clear · Local · Authoring · Reading · Access "Clarity" is squarely on-brand for low vision Medium-high — common given name + Clara.io / Clara AI

Resonant but avoid

  • ARIA — reads as Accessible Reading, Input & Authoring, and WAI-ARIA is the accessibility standard, so the resonance is perfect — but that is exactly why it is not ownable and is confusing. Great metaphor, bad trademark.
  • PRISM / PRISMA — a prism splits one light into many (one document → text, speech, braille) is a lovely metaphor, but collisions are heavy (Prisma ORM, Adobe) and "Prism" is already used internally for the screen-reader bridge. Keep as copy, not a name.
  • NOVA / AURA / AVID / SAGE / VISTA — all spell nicely and fit, but each is an established software / AI / media product (or Windows Vista). Distinctiveness too low.

Pick order for distinctiveness + clean field + on-meaning: AVRA, then VERBA, then LUMA (if it clears). AVRA and VERBA are likeliest to survive a Class 9 search because they are coined or Latin rather than a trendy English tech term.

Heritage note: TINDRA is the evolution of QUILL by Community Access — same mission (writing for everyone, private and on-device), a name we can own.
